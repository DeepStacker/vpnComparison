A VPN, or Virtual Private Network, creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a remote server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic is routed through this tunnel, hiding your IP address and encrypting your data from anyone who might be watching — your ISP, the coffee shop Wi-Fi operator, or a hacker on the same network.

## How a VPN Works

When you connect to a VPN:

1. **Your device establishes a secure connection** to the VPN server using an encrypted protocol like WireGuard, OpenVPN, or IKEv2.
2. **Your real IP address is replaced** with the VPN server's IP address, making it appear as though you're browsing from the server's location.
3. **All data is encrypted** before leaving your device and decrypted at the VPN server, preventing anyone on your network from reading your traffic.
4. **The VPN server forwards your request** to the website or service you're accessing, then routes the response back through the encrypted tunnel.

## Why Use a VPN?

- **Privacy** — Your ISP cannot see which websites you visit or what you download.
- **Security** — Your data is encrypted on public Wi-Fi networks.
- **Geo-unblocking** — Access content that's restricted in your region (requires a VPN that supports streaming).
- **Avoid throttling** — ISPs sometimes slow down specific types of traffic; encryption prevents them from identifying traffic types.

## What a VPN Does NOT Do

A VPN is not a complete privacy solution. It won't prevent websites from tracking you via cookies, browser fingerprinting, or logged-in accounts. It won't make you anonymous — your VPN provider can still see your traffic if they log it. It won't protect you from malware or phishing attacks.
